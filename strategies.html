Current Strategies

Mastercard have several initiatives in place to address a subset of the UN SDG’s. Their largest current and most recent initiatives are outlined and reviewed below.

Goal 2: Zero Hunger

Mastercard formed a partnership with the World Food Programme (WFP) in 2012, with the partnership still ongoing to this day. As part of this partnership, alongside local non-profit organisations, they committed to provide 100 million school meals. The goal was achieved in its desired 2-year timeframe, by year end 2019, with 107 million meals provided (Mastercard Incorporated, 2019). The initiative could be regarded as a success, particularly when factoring in the flow on effect of providing meals. This effect is the direct positive impact providing meals has on both reducing poverty (goal 1) and enhancing education (goal 4). The school meal is often the different between working in the fields with the parents or at home, or attending school, hence the direct educational benefit. This has a longer-term benefit of developing self-sufficiency amongst communities.

Historically, one factor behind food shortages is not the lack of food itself, but rather war and conflict. The relationship between food insecurity and conflict is highly complex; food shortages can cause conflict but by the same token conflict can cause food shortages, and an unrelenting cycle can result (Messer & Cohen, 2015). Thus, the school meals provided may have a further flow on effect to reduced conflict when factoring in the increased education levels and reduced food shortage.

A cost-benefit analysis into school feeding programs conducted in 2018 found for every US $1 invested, the economic return is US $6.2 (Kolesova & Moataz, 2018). Growing the economy results in an indirect long-term benefit to Mastercard if this leads to a growth in the digital economy. Future works by the company in relation to goal 2 should also consider steps towards reducing food wastage. Concluding remarks are that the success of the program can likely be attributed to the effective use of partnerships, which should be considered when developing future initiatives.

Goal 5: Gender Equality

Mastercard has a commitment to gender equality both internally, and externally. In 2018, the company reported women earnt $0.996 for every $1.00 earnt by men. In 2019, this figure had changed to women earning $1.00 for every $1.00 earnt by men, with this ratio maintained in the company’s most recently reported data. In addition, as a technology company Mastercard plays a role in helping to address the highly prevalent gender inequality and stigma within the science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) sector. They implemented the successful Girls4Tech program, having reached 1 million 8- to 16-year-olds since commencing in 2014, with the intent to encourage STEM uptake and consider it as a career avenue (Mastercard Incorporated, 2020).

Collectively these initiatives demonstrate that Mastercard has followed through on their proposals, with actions not just the intent. The benefit to the company is developing a stronger reputation as an equal opportunity employer and thus more likely to attract stronger candidates when recruiting.

Goal 8 Decent Work and Economic Growth

Mastercard has publicised large donations to supporting COVID-19 economic recovery. In addition, they have a commitment to bring 1 billion people, and 50 million small and micro-businesses into the digital economy within the next 4 years. Developing the digital economy has a direct positive benefit to Mastercard’s bottom line.

Goal 11: Sustainable Cities and Communities

Globally, cities represent the largest source of gross-domestic product (GDP) and ergo a large source of revenue for Mastercard. The City Possibly program launched by Mastercard strives to develop “sustainable urban futures” (City Possible, 2021). The program has 220 city members and provides a streamlined method for collaboration between public companies, private companies, academics, and communities. The merit of the program is the long-term focus, adopting a grounds-up approach to strive for meaningful change.

Goal 17: partnerships

In addition to the aforementioned partnerships, Mastercard collaboratively launched data.org. This platform utilises their expertise and datasets to make them more accessible to non-profit, government and civic organisations. The intent is for the organisations to use the datasets for social impact. Data-driven decisions can provide companies with a competitive advantage (Arunachalam & Kumar, 2018). Having this insight accessible to aid in making informed decisions has widescale benefits in a diverse spread of government and non-profit organisations. The advantage to Mastercard is the relationships formed and an increase in goodwill.
