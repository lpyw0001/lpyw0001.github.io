Recommendations

Climate change is the aftermath of two competing agendas, the pursuit of economic growth coupled with a general disregard of the environmental and resource challenges this pursuit requires (Wolf, 2012). Successfully finding the middle ground between these two visions is a complex political, technological challenge, without even factoring in the time pressure. Climate change has direct ramifications on numerous sectors: water, wildlife, agriculture, human health, energy, and transportation, amongst others (National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, 2019). This complex interrelationship, and the impact climate change can have on the accomplishment of other sustainable development goals is the primary reason for focusing on goal 13 and examining the role Mastercard can play in developing additional strategies to complement their current array of initiatives.

Inaction towards climate change impact on GDP (OECD, 2015)

Modelling by OECD found inaction towards climate change would have direct economic impacts, a decline in GDP (OECD, 2015). A weak economy would likely mean a decrease in revenue for Mastercard due to a corresponding reduction in revenue collected from switching and volume fees. Moreover, a growing number of investors review the climate related financial disclosures provided by companies and use a means to evaluate investment decision. Thus, there is an additional financial incentive for the pursuit of goal 13, to attract additional investors, and ceteris paribus increase the share price further.

Carbon removal technology

Aside from the priceless planet initiative in place to help with carbon removal from the environment, it is recommended for Mastercard to invest in Carbon removal technology. This is because these technologies are considered as an active way for carbon emissions removal which can accelerate decarbonization efforts. Based on the Paris Agreement, to avoid alarming global warming, global temperature must be kept to a 1.5-degree increase (World Resources Institute [WRI], n.d.). WRI further stated that to achieve this goal, it needs to remove 10 Gt CO2 per year and 20 Gt CO2 per year by 2050 and 2100 respectively. Furthermore, World Meteorological Organisation (WMO) warned that there is a 20% chance that temperature could rise above 1.5% as early as 2024 (United Nations Environment Programme, n.d.). Therefore, technological advancement alongside the traditional tree restorations is needed to maximize carbon emission removal.

By implementing this strategy, Mastercard can utilize the ‘domino effect’ highly prevalent in the technology sector. This is because Mastercard is relatively indistinguishable from its largest competitor, Visa, when one company introduces a feature or service, the other often follows. Hence, Mastercard will be able to directly challenge its competitors to follow in their footsteps. There are many carbon technologies available including bioenergy with carbon capture and direct air capture (Bistline & Blanford, 2021). Direct air capture is currently the most promising, this method captures CO2 from the atmosphere and stores it underground (WRI, n.d.). Compared to bioenergy with carbon capture, direct air capture has a higher sequestration efficiency (Cuffari, 2021). One of the most significant plants for this technology, can capture about 13,000 metric tons (Hiar,2021). One current limiting factor is the cost, currently ranging from $250-$600 per tonne of CO2 captured (Lebling, McQueen, Pisciotta & Wilcox, 2021). However, with government incentives and funding, and, benefitting from technological spill-over effects, the cost can be reduced (International Energy Agency, 2020).

The carbon removal industry is still in relatively infancy, and whilst not a first mover, as an early investor Mastercard can benefit from the research performed so far and profit from future breakthroughs by investing capital. Doing so would help Mastercard to take a step ahead of their direct competitors and actively contribute to carbon emissions removal.

Political pressure

Another strategy that Mastercard could implement is to place political pressure on governments that put minimal effort into climate change initiatives. This political pressure could come in the form of a threat to not process any credit card transactions if certain climate change commitments are not made. Whilst this strategy may result in Mastercard seen as bold and forward acting, it would encounter significant backlash from the public, businesses, and the government, due to A) the inconvenience it would cause and B) the potential revenue loss for businesses. Thus, Mastercard would have no intent to follow through, due to the scope of possible repercussions. The intent behind the strategy is that as per the United Nations, governments play a vital role in driving a systemic shift to addressing climate change (United Nations [UN], n.d.). Some actions governments could be required to commit to include the elimination of fossil fuel subsidies, and quantifying and confronting climate associated risks, among others. In exchange for meeting these commitments Mastercard can offer to invest in long-term energy storage projects locally in that country, which aids in creating a low-carbon economy as consumption continues to increase (Climate Investment Funds, 2021).

Renewable energy

Cambridge University Press published a book that claims, “Renewable energy sources play a role in providing energy services in a sustainable manner and, in particular, in mitigating climate change” (Edenhofer et al., 2011). Mastercard’s largest energy contributor are their data centres (2020 report). Mastercard currently claims 100% renewable energy status, however currently 88.5% of these is through the purchase of credits. Hence to reduce reliance on these credits, and simultaneously contribute to goal 13 Mastercard could provide additional on-site renewable energy generation and storage at these data centres. The initial capital outlay would only be modest, and provides environmental benefits, and future cash flows from the generated electricity. In addition to providing on-site generation, Mastercard could invest capital in new large-scale energy storage projects, such as green hydrogen, batteries, and compressed air energy storage systems, that have attached carbon dioxide capture systems (Zeynalian, Hajialirezaei, Razmi, & Torabi, 2020). As an early investor in new energy storage projects, Mastercard has the potential to generate higher returns from the investment. Furthermore, renewable energy sources are now cheaper than fossil fuels which can help Mastercard cut their costs in the long run (International renewable energy agency [IREA], 2021), with costs of new solar projects ~85% of their prior cost (Masterson, 2021). Another factor that favours this recommendation is the public funding and financial incentives for renewable energy that the U.S Governments have laid out (Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.). By transitioning to using more renewable energy sources, it could drive further economic growth as it gives more employment opportunities and increase human welfare (IREA, 2016) which benefits Mastercard in the long run. Given the positive trend in political, technological factors and potential economic benefits, Mastercard is recommended to start and continue investing in more renewable energy sources for its data centres and slowly apply it to their other departments.

Shared Economy

A final strategy Mastercard can implement is to incentivise a transition to more of a shared economy. Precise definitions of a shared economy vary; however, the common theme is the sharing of both goods and services, facilitated via a digital platform (Fors, Inutsuka, & Orito, 2021). For example, Uber and Airbnb. The value of this platform is the ability to increase resource efficiency, fundamentally alter consumption habits, have less ownership, and less idle capacity, and therefore reduced environmental impacts and emissions (Perez-Perez, Benito-Osorio, Garcia-Moreno, & Martinez-Fernandez, 2021). An up-and-coming example of this is reduced car ownership as vehicles gradually become more autonomous. Mastercard could utilise their existing network infrastructure to partner with an autonomous vehicle company and incentivise, for example short-term discount, the use of the platform.
