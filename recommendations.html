Recommendations

Climate change is the aftermath of two competing agendas, the pursuit of economic growth coupled with a general disregard of the environmental and resource challenges this pursuit requires (Wolf, 2012). Successfully finding the middle ground between these two visions is a complex political, technological challenge, without even factoring in the time pressure. Climate change has direct ramifications on numerous sectors: water, wildlife, agriculture, human health, energy, and transportation, amongst others (National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, 2019). This complex interrelationship is the primary reason for focusing on goal 13 and examining the role Mastercard can play in developing additional strategies to complement their current array of initiatives.

Modelling by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) found inaction towards climate change would have direct economic impacts, causing a decline in GDP (OECD, 2015). A weak economy would mean a decrease in revenue for Mastercard due to a corresponding reduction in revenue collected from switching and volume fees. Moreover, a growing number of investors review the climate related financial disclosures provided by companies and use them to evaluate investment decisions. Thus, there is an additional financial incentive for the pursuit of goal 13, to attract additional investors, and ceteris paribus increase the share price further.

Carbon removal technology

To complement the priceless planet initiative already in place, it is recommended for Mastercard to invest in carbon removal technology. These technologies can accelerate decarbonization efforts. To limiting global warming to a 1.5°C increase, 10 Gt of CO2 is required to be removed each year till 2050. Furthermore, the World Meteorological Organisation (WMO) warned there is a 20% chance that temperature could rise above 1.5°C as early as 2024 (United Nations Environment Programme, n.d.). Therefore, technological advancement alongside the traditional tree restorations is needed to maximize carbon emission removal.

By implementing this strategy, Mastercard can utilize the ‘domino effect’ highly prevalent in the technology sector. This is because Mastercard is relatively indistinguishable from its largest competitor, Visa, when one company introduces a feature or service, the other often follows. Hence, Mastercard will be able to directly challenge its competitors to follow in their footsteps.

Some potential carbon removal technologies available include bioenergy with carbon capture and direct air capture (DIA)(Bistline & Blanford, 2021). DIA is currently the most promising; this method captures CO2 from the atmosphere and stores it underground (WRI, n.d.). Compared to bioenergy with carbon capture, DIA has a higher sequestration efficiency (Cuffari, 2021). Currently it can capture about 13,000 metric tons (Hiar,2021). A limiting factor is the cost, ranging from $250-$600 per tonne of CO2 captured (Lebling, McQueen, Pisciotta & Wilcox, 2021). However, with government incentives and funding, and, benefitting from technological spill-over effects, this cost can be reduced (International Energy Agency, 2020).

The carbon removal industry is still in relatively infancy, and whilst not a first mover, as an early investor Mastercard can benefit from the research performed so far and profit from future breakthroughs by investing capital. Doing so would help Mastercard take a step ahead of their direct competitors and actively contribute to carbon emissions removal.

Political pressure

Another strategy Mastercard can implement is to place political pressure on governments that put minimal effort into climate change initiatives. This political pressure could come in the form of a threat to not process any credit card transactions for a short timeframe if certain climate change commitments are not made. This strategy would almost certainly encounter significant backlash from the public, businesses, and the government. This is due to both the inconvenience it would cause and the potential revenue losses for businesses. Thus, Mastercard would have no intent to follow through, due to the scope of possible repercussions.

The intent behind the strategy is that as per the United Nations, governments play a vital role in driving a systemic shift to addressing climate change (United Nations [UN], n.d.). Some actions governments could be required to commit to include the elimination of fossil fuel subsidies, and quantifying and confronting climate associated risks, among others. In exchange for meeting these commitments Mastercard can offer to invest in long-term energy storage projects locally in that country, which aids in creating a low-carbon economy as consumption continues to increase (Climate Investment Funds, 2021).

Renewable energy

Cambridge University Press published a book that claims,

“Renewable energy sources play a role in providing energy services in a sustainable manner and, in particular, in mitigating climate change” (Edenhofer et al., 2011).
Mastercard’s largest energy contributor are their data centres (Mastercard Incorporated, 2020). Whilst Mastercard claims 100% renewable energy status, currently 88.5% of this is through the purchase of credits. To reduce reliance on these credits, and simultaneously contribute to goal 13 Mastercard could provide additional on-site renewable energy generation and storage at these data centres. The initial capital outlay would only be modest, and provides environmental benefits, and future cash flows from the generated electricity. Mastercard could also invest capital in new large-scale energy storage projects, such as green hydrogen, batteries, and compressed air energy storage systems, that have attached carbon dioxide capture systems (Zeynalian, Hajialirezaei, Razmi, & Torabi, 2020). As an early investor in new energy storage projects, Mastercard has the potential to generate higher returns from the investment. Furthermore, renewable energy sources are now cheaper than fossil fuels which can help Mastercard cut costs in the long-run (International renewable energy agency [IREA], 2021). Another factor that favours this recommendation is the public funding and financial incentives for renewable energy that the U.S Governments have laid out (Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.). Transitioning to using more renewable energy sources can drive further economic growth as it gives more employment opportunities and increased human welfare (IREA, 2016) which benefits Mastercard in the long run.

Shared Economy

A final strategy Mastercard can implement is to incentivise a transition to a shared economy. Precise definitions of a shared economy vary; however, the common theme is the sharing of both goods and services, facilitated via a digital platform (Fors, Inutsuka, & Orito, 2021). For example, Uber and Airbnb. The value of this platform is the ability to increase resource efficiency, alter consumption habits, have less ownership, and less idle capacity, and therefore reduced environmental impacts and emissions (Perez-Perez, Benito-Osorio, Garcia-Moreno, & Martinez-Fernandez, 2021). An up-and-coming example of this is reduced car ownership as vehicles gradually become more autonomous. Mastercard could utilise their existing network infrastructure to partner with an autonomous vehicle company and incentivise, for example short-term discount, the use of the platform. Mastercard would then generate revenue from all transactions processed as part of the partnership.
