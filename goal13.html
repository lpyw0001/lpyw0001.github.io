Goal 13

"Take urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts"

According to a recent statement from the United Nations,

“Climate change is affecting every country on every continent. It is disrupting national economies and affecting lives. Weather patterns are changing, sea levels are rising, and weather events are becoming more extreme… Saving lives and livelihoods requires urgent action to address both the pandemic and the climate emergency.” (United Nations, 2021)

Climate change has the potential to cause food scarcity and hence reduce the effectiveness of millions of dollars of already invested capital in other developmental programs. Thus, taking steps to mitigate climate change is required to help ensure the long-term success of not just climate related initiatives, but a much broader range of developmental strategies. Further to this, according to the Royal Australian College of General Practitioners:

“Climate change resulting from human activity is affecting our relationship with our environment and presents an urgent, significant and growing threat to health worldwide” (The Royal Australian College of General Practitioners, 2021)

Between the priceless planet initiative and commitment to net zero emissions Mastercard have demonstrated a strong commitment to supporting their employee health and wellbeing by taking action to mitigate climate change and leading the way in sustainability infrastructure.

Priceless Planet Coalition

Higgs (2014) in her book “Collision Course: Endless Growth on a Finite Planet” unpacks the complexities of pursuing economic growth and the potential ramifications for the planet. Mastercard recognises this complex relationship and have several initiatives in place to combat climate change. Late last year Mastercard launched the Priceless Planet Coalition, with the goal to restore 100 million trees by 2025, with the trees acting as a natural method of carbon capture. Since forming, the coalition now consists of 60 partners.

Planting trees may often be seen as a “be-all and end-all” solution to climate change, however this is not factoring in trees can be a natural heat trap, further exacerbating the global warming issue (Marshall, 2020). Thus, the location the trees are planted will be pivotal to the program’s success. Supporting this argument is a recent article by Kimbrough (2021) concluding that trees planted in locations where they are not usually grown, can do more harm than good in terms of carbon capture and release. Mastercard have demonstrated a recognition of this issue by enlisting climate experts to closely examine the ideal planting locations to have the greatest potential positive impact towards the climate, the community and biodiversity. Australia is one of the initial locations for reforestation efforts, with forests destroyed by recent bushfires (external factor) and an estimated 900 million tonnes of carbon dioxide released into the atmosphere. This amount is almost twice Australia’s total yearly emissions (Chow, 2020). The remaining two locations for initial works are Kenya (Makueni County) and Brazil (Amazon and Atlantic forests).

The collaborative initiative, driven by consumers, financial institutions, merchants, and cities serves a few key roles, unpacked below:

  1. Inspire collective action

    2. Mastercard have effectively utilised their network effect, to help empower their 3 billion consumer and corporate cardholders to take action to mitigate climate change. They provided a platform for individuals or companies to donate to the initiative. They have collaborated with several companies to allow rewards points to be redeemed for trees. In addition, Mastercard collaborated with Doconomy, to develop a platform for individuals to calculate and take accountability for their carbon footprint.

    As shown in a recent Mastercard survey, 58 percent of consumers are more aware of their environmental impact, and 85 percent ready to take personal action this year (Mastercard Newsroom, 2021).

    The partnership allows for Mastercard transactions to be directly translated to their environmental impact (Doconomy, 2021). This initiative aligns with a general increase in consumer interest and concern around their environmental impact and provides them a quantitative assessment. This increased education may help to inspire further collective action. Moreover, the COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the dangers of a rapid spread of misinformation (Moshirnia, 2021), thus Mastercard’s educational efforts may help to reduce further spread of misinformation in relation to climate change, thus adding further merit to their collaboration.

  2. Reduce the risk and severity of the consequences associated with climate change inaction

    3. Through the tree restoration efforts.

In relation to the coalition’s success and merits, firstly a recent study published in Springer Nature found that nature-based solutions to addressing climate change can be effective when they have a clear long-term focus and are coupled with other methods (Girardin et al., 2021). On this basis the initiative has merit provided it is not a standalone program. In addition, the initial groundwork appears promising but with tree planting works only commencing this year the program’s long-term success will require review a later stage. Finally, Mastercard have effectively used the “100 million” number to capture public attention and gain traction, however going forward the focus should be less on the number of trees planted and migrate to keeping the trees alive and healthy long-term.

Commitment to Net Zero Emissions

Mastercard have a public commitment to be carbon neutral in 2021 and net zero emissions by 2050 (Mastercard Incorporated, 2020). To help accomplish this, all Mastercard’s owned buildings are certified by the Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED). This is a great starting point, however ‘certified’ is the minimum requirement, Mastercard should endeavour to accrue more points and work towards the gold or platinum certification (LEED, 2021). As part of accruing addition points Mastercard could investigate the role green hydrogen can play (Ewing, Israel, Jutt, Talebian, & Stepanik, 2020). Further efforts to achieve net zero emissions include the use of 100% renewable energy, aiming for 100% recycling of electronic waste, utilising science-based targets, issuing a $600M sustainability bond, partnering and working towards sustainable cryptocurrencies, and engaging suppliers to reduce greenhouse gas emissions along their complete supply chain. Quantitatively, Mastercard found that their suppliers accounted for 70% of the total footprint, thus this initiative to actively engage with suppliers could prove effective (Mastercard Incorporated, 2020). In relation to the above figures however, the 2020 and 2021 figures should be updated to include the emissions that are resultant from employees working remotely/from home that should be directly attributed back to Mastercard. Thus, the emissions reduction in 2020 may not be entirely indicative of the ‘true’ emissions reduction if these additional emissions are not quantified.

As a further measure to achieve this net zero commitment, Mastercard have coupled executive compensation with the achievement of carbon neutrality in 2021. According to a recent report by PwC, this practice is becoming increasingly common, but quantitative data to evaluate its effectiveness is limited, as it is a relatively new practice (O’Connor, Harris, & Gosling, 2021). Mastercard have evidently overcome any backlash this move generated internally. However, whilst this provides a tangible incentive, Mastercard needs to ensure appropriately calibrated requirements and ensure the broad long-term goal is not lost in distorted short-term incentives.

Critical to Mastercard’s commitment is the word ‘net’ in net zero emissions. The implication here is whether, from a practical standpoint, an indefinite quantity of carbon emissions can be offset. This is a key factor, that reporting only ‘net’ emissions can be misleading and may be inadequate. Rather, the emissions should be in two distinct categories, that of negative emissions, and emissions reduction (McLaren, 2019). Negative emissions technology is currently an unproven technology, and some proposed geoengineering methods, to inject sulphuric acid into the stratosphere, have an associated higher risk and ethical concerns. Thus, to achieve their goal Mastercard will likely be required to reduce emissions further as opposed to relying on credits and carbon removal strategies. This will be reviewed in more detail in the following section. Going forward, to demonstrate accountability, Mastercard should clearly delineate the net change in each of these two distinct categories. This reporting change will likely appease shareholders given “increasing evidence in the academic literature that sustainability has a positive impact on the bottom line and shareholder value” (O’Connor et al., 2021).
